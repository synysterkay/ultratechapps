## How to Write a Thesis Statement: A 5-Step Formula

Follow this proven, step-by-step process to build a powerful thesis every time.

**Quick Answer:** To write a strong thesis statement, start with your essay prompt or research question. Form a preliminary opinion, then refine it into a specific, arguable claim that outlines your main points. Finally, test it for clarity and strength before beginning your outline.

### Step 1: Start with a Question
Begin by turning your assignment prompt or broad topic into a specific question. If your prompt is "Discuss the effects of social media," reformulate it to "What is the impact of social media on the mental health of teenagers?" This question provides a clear direction for your research and initial argument.

### Step 2: Write a Preliminary Answer
After initial reading, draft a simple answer to your question. Don‚Äôt worry about perfection here. For our example, a first attempt might be: "Social media has a negative impact on teenagers' mental health." This is your working thesis‚Äîa starting point for refinement.

### Step 3: Develop Your Answer and Include Reasoning
Why do you believe your preliminary answer is true? Develop your answer by adding the "because" clause. This builds the logic of your argument. Example: "Social media has a largely negative impact on teenagers' mental health *because it promotes unrealistic social comparison and enables cyberbullying.*"

### Step 4: Refine for Specificity and Scope
A strong thesis is specific. Replace vague terms with precise language. Specify the platforms, demographics, or outcomes. Refined example: "Frequent use of image-centric platforms like Instagram and TikTok contributes to increased anxiety and body dissatisfaction among adolescent girls by fostering constant social comparison."

### Step 5: Test and Finalize Your Thesis Statement
Put your thesis through this final checklist:
*   **Is it arguable?** Could a reasonable person disagree?
*   **Is it specific?** Does it avoid vague words like "good," "bad," or "many"?
   *   **Is it manageable?** Can it be supported within the scope of your essay?
   *   **Does it pass the "So What?" test?** Does it convey the importance of your argument?

If it passes, you have a strong, actionable thesis.

### Can a thesis statement be two sentences?
Yes, a complex argument sometimes requires two sentences. However, strive for conciseness. Often, what seems to need two sentences can be combined into one more powerful, complex sentence using clauses (e.g., "although... because...").

### What's the difference between a thesis statement and a topic sentence?
A **thesis statement** presents the main argument for your entire essay. A **topic sentence** presents the main idea of a single body paragraph. Each topic sentence should directly support and link back to a part of your overarching thesis.

### How specific does my thesis need to be?
Extremely specific. It should narrow the topic down to a manageable scope for your essay length. Instead of "pollution is bad," try "Single-use plastic policies in coastal cities significantly reduce microplastic pollution in local marine ecosystems."
